01

Context

The UAE is one of the world's most active fintech markets, in a distinctive banking culture. Conventional neobanks — Liv., Wio, YAP — have set a high bar for digital experience, while Islamic incumbents such as ADIB and Dubai Islamic Bank hold deep trust and Sharia-compliance but often run on older, friction-heavy apps. In practice, observant users tend to face a quiet trade-off between their values and a polished experience.

I took this on as a self-initiated project, to explore what closing that gap might look like for a user the market rarely designs for natively: the Emirati national, for whom Arabic is the language of anything serious, and for whom Sharia-compliance is a matter of identity rather than a feature.

~89%of UAE residents are expats — leaving Emirati citizens a smaller, underserved premium segment
12.5M+users on Aani, the UAE's instant-payment rail, by 2026
2.5%the Zakat rate — an annual obligation most banking apps aren't designed around
02

First, the world this app lives in

To judge the design you need the vocabulary it's built on. Islamic finance isn't "banking with a green logo" — it's a different model with real product consequences. Here's the plain-language version.

Zakat زكاة

One of the five pillars of Islam: an obligatory annual charity of 2.5% on the wealth a Muslim has held for a full lunar year, once it passes a minimum threshold. It purifies wealth and redistributes it to those in need.

Nisab نِصاب

The threshold Zakat begins at — the value of about 85g of gold or 595g of silver. Below it, no Zakat is due. Because it's pegged to metal prices, the figure moves daily.

Hawl حول

The one lunar year wealth must stay above Nisab before Zakat is owed. The Hijri year is ~354 days — shorter than the Gregorian — so reminders are best timed to the moon, not the calendar.

Riba ربا

The prohibition of interest. A fixed, guaranteed return on lent money is not permitted — which is why the app speaks of shared profit, never "interest".

Mudarabah مضاربة

The permitted alternative to interest: profit-sharing. The bank invests a deposit and shares the real profit by an agreed ratio. No fixed rate, no guarantee — the return follows actual performance.

The 8 asnaf المصارف الثمانية

The eight categories of people named in the Qur'an who may receive Zakat — the poor, the needy, those in debt, the wayfarer, and others. Where Zakat may go is itself a religious matter.

Sukuk صكوك

"Islamic bonds" — but rather than paying interest, they represent ownership of real assets and pay returns from those assets. A permitted way to invest and earn.

Sadaqah & Waqf صدقة · وقف

Sadaqah is voluntary charity — any amount, any time. Waqf is an endowment, an asset held so its proceeds give in perpetuity. Both sit alongside Zakat as distinct acts of giving.

Why this matters for design

Each of these concepts becomes an interface decision: live-priced Nisab, a lunar reminder engine, the word "profit" in place of "interest", a multi-asset calculator, and giving routed to verified recipients within the eight asnaf.

The product needed to reflect fiqh (Islamic jurisprudence), not simply visualise balances.

Desk research — the rails and the rules

Before any pixels, I mapped the national systems a UAE banking app plugs into. Three rails shaped onboarding, payments and the Zakat engine.

UAE PASSNational identity, 11M+ users. Instant, verified onboarding — no selfie.
AaniCentral-bank instant payments, 12.5M+ users. Pay by mobile number in ~3s.
AAOIFI goldShariah-compliant gold, mandatory since 2018. Counts toward Zakat.

Sources: CBUAE & Al Etihad Payments · TDRA / ICP · World Gold Council / AAOIFI · Emirates Islamic.

Insight

Onboarding by Emirates-ID scan and selfie is table-stakes. UAE PASS — which most Emiratis already have — tends to be used by competitors for login, not account opening.

So onboarding leads with UAE PASS: near-instant, no selfie, and built for citizens.
On rigour & honesty: figures above are drawn from public sources (Central Bank of the UAE, Al Etihad Payments, TDRA/ICP, World Gold Council, and app-store listings). This is a concept project: I had no quantitative access, so I triangulated secondary research, competitive teardowns, app-store review mining and four informal expert conversations — and I label directional insight as directional, never as statistics.

06

Reading the results

Across the primer, the desk research, the teardown and the reviews, the same five conclusions kept surfacing — each triangulated from more than one source.

01

Trust is lost after acquisition, not during it

Onboarding is praised almost everywhere; the complaints cluster on reliability, support, refunds and frozen accounts. The weak point isn't getting users in — it's holding their confidence once money is moving.

Read from: review mining · competitive teardown
02

Sharia-compliance is marketed, not engineered

Only ADIB and Emirates Islamic ship real Islamic tooling; DIB has no in-app Zakat calculator, and the neobanks none at all. "Compliant" is mostly a label — which leaves genuine product depth wide open.

Read from: competitive teardown
03

Craft and compliance never appear together

The radar shapes make it plain: Islamic apps are thin on craft, neobanks thin on Islamic depth. No product holds both, so the intersection is unclaimed rather than crowded.

Read from: scoring radars
04

Arabic is shipped everywhere, designed nowhere

Every app offers Arabic, yet none demonstrably treats RTL as first-class design — it's the one dimension I couldn't even score. That makes it a quiet craft advantage, not a feature war.

Read from: RTL audit
05

Zakat is the biggest emotional moment and the smallest toolset

Zakat is annual, manual, lunar-timed and anxiety-laden — yet barely supported in-app. The highest-meaning moment in a Muslim user's financial year is exactly where the tooling is weakest.

Read from: Islamic-finance primer · user behaviour

Put together, they point one way:

Onboarding is already table-stakes. The opportunity lies in treating Islamic finance as genuine product depth, in first-class Arabic RTL, and in transparent money handling — and, for an Emirati user, in UAE PASS onboarding that competitors haven't yet adopted.

Emirati · Abu Dhabi · senior government professional · married, two children

"I want a bank that respects my faith and my time. Right now I get one or the other."

Goals
  • Bank as smoothly as a Wio user, without compromising her values
  • Pay Zakat correctly and on time, across all her assets
  • Give meaningfully — and see it actually reach people
Frustrations
  • Islamic apps feel dated and buggy; neobanks aren't halal
  • Calculates Zakat by hand once a year, unsure she's counting gold right
  • Arabic in finance apps feels machine-flipped, not designed
Context & behaviour

Bilingual, but switches to Arabic the moment money gets serious. Already has UAE PASS. Holds part of her wealth in physical gold — inherited and gifted — that sits idle and complicates her Zakat. Her trigger moment is Ramadan, as her Hawl date approaches and the annual Zakat anxiety returns.

How might we…

  • make Zakat continuously visible and effortless across all assets, so it stops being an annual panic?
  • make giving feel verified and meaningful, not a transfer into a void?
  • deliver neobank-grade polish while making compliance a visible, trustworthy product?
  • make Arabic-first RTL the premium default, not an afterthought?
  • build trust through transparency, where incumbents lose it?

Smart Zakat — turning an obligation into a relationship

The research suggested Zakat is an annual, manual and often anxious ritual, made harder by multi-asset wealth and lunar timing. I approached it not as a form to fill in but as something the bank helps with through the year.

Insight → decision

Hessa calculates Zakat once a year, and isn't always sure she's counting gold and investments correctly.

Track wealth against a live-priced Nisab through the year, with a "Zakat health" status, a Hijri Hawl countdown and a timeline — so the obligation stays visible rather than surfacing as a year-end scramble.

12

Directions I rejected

Before committing to Zakat & giving as the hero, I scoped other directions and set them aside. The reasoning matters as much as the choice.

×Remittance-first app

The most relatable expat need — but a crowded, price-driven race (Wio, Botim, the banks) that sidesteps the Islamic-finance depth that is actually my edge.

×Youth / teen banking

Open and playful, but a weaker seniority signal — and ADIB's Amwali already owns the Islamic-youth niche.

×A full super-app

Tempting for scope, but Liv.'s over-reach showed it buries clarity. I chose depth on one meaningful flow over breadth.

×Gold-savings as the hero

Culturally strong, and it stayed in the build — but Zakat read as the higher-emotion, more distinctive story, so it leads instead.

13

How I'd validate & measure it

I scoped this as a concept to pressure-test the strategy and the craft. Validation with users is the next phase — and I defined the plan and the success metrics up front, so the bar is set beforehand rather than after the fact. It draws on the known drivers of Islamic-fintech adoption: Sharia-compliance, ease of use, perceived benefit, Islamic Social Responsibility and trust.

Method

5–7 moderated usability sessions, Arabic-first, remote and in-person across Abu Dhabi and Dubai.

Participants

Emirati Muslims who pay Zakat and hold some wealth in gold; a spread of ages and digital confidence.

Key tasks

Onboard via UAE PASS; assemble assets and read the Zakat figure; choose the Nisab basis; give across the eight asnaf.

What I'd measure

Task completion and time, comprehension of "profit, not interest", trust in the Zakat number, reception of the du'a moment, and the Western-vs-Arabic-Indic numerals preference.

≥20%DAU/MAU target — a strong ratio for fintech
≥80%target completion of the Zakat flow, unaided
<2 minonboarding to first live Zakat estimate
Set before testing

Defining the tasks and metrics in advance is deliberate — success is measured against a bar set beforehand, not graded on a curve afterwards. The discussion guide is ready to run; I'd revise the hero flow on the findings, with the Zakat content scholar-reviewed before any release.

14

Scope, constraints & feasibility

A real product lives inside constraints; designing as if they didn't exist is the giveaway of a concept. Here's the reality I designed against — and how each shaped a decision.

Regulatory path

For an Islamic retail product I'd target the CBUAE mainland framework, with a DFSA Innovation Testing Licence pilot in the DIFC as a faster route to test with live users. Either way, KYC runs on Emirates ID / UAE PASS — which is why onboarding is built around them.

Sharia governance

"Sharia-compliant" isn't a checkbox: it needs a Sharia Supervisory Board and AAOIFI-aligned controls, with the DFSA acting as a "Shari'a systems regulator". That's why compliance is a visible, certified product layer — "profit, not interest", and on-screen assurance — not a badge.

Build model

An MVP would most realistically launch on a partner-bank / BaaS licence (as YAP did with Ruya) rather than a full bank licence — keeping the first release tight and shippable.

The wider shift

This rides rails arriving fast: Open Finance (the CBUAE's AlTareq; ADIB is the first licensed TPP), fractional "Smart" Sukuk from ~$1,000, and early tokenisation pilots — the roadmap beyond the MVP.

On staying in my lane

I'm a designer, not a compliance or platform lead — but designing without these in view produces work that can't ship. Knowing the terrain is what made the trade-offs — UAE PASS-first, visible compliance, a tight MVP — deliberate rather than accidental.

15

Reflection & next steps

Treating this as a concept was the point: the aim is to show how I read an unfamiliar, high-context market and turn it into product decisions — market understanding, research and RTL craft. Validation with users is the defined next phase (the plan and metrics are in §13), and the Zakat content would be scholar-reviewed before any release.

In my portfolio this sits alongside shipped, team-delivered work; here the intent was to show how I'd approach an unfamiliar, high-context market from first principles.
