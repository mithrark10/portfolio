The challenge

Everyone had an opinion on AI in design. Nobody had evidence.

So I ran a POC to settle it with proof — on one deliberately messy, high-stakes workflow: an appointment-scheduling app for a drug-rehab facility.

Decisions I made

Five calls that shaped how the POC ran.

01
The setup

Split the team into two pods

Design-first vs code-first — the only way to see how far AI plugs into a real designer's workflow.

02
The scope

Kept the brief deliberately small

Let the work surface itself. It narrowed to two screens: the appointment calendar and the form.

03
The baseline

Locked one prompting standard

Handpicked the patterns that worked, so both pods built from the same footing — not eight different ones.

04
The hard test

Narrowed the design system to one screen

The appointment-creation form — a fair, focused battleground for the toughest part.

05
The proof

Made each pod integrate its own system

Rebuild the screen on the design system you generated. The real test of AI end to end.

The findings

AI sped up the start — not the judgment inside.

Where AI accelerated

What went well

  • Faster personas, user flows, and workflows
  • Faster research synthesis and documentation
  • Quick exploration of scenarios and edge cases
  • Less repetitive analysis and doc effort
  • Faster jump from discovery to design
  • Faster hi-fi wireframes and UI concepts
  • Early visualization before heavy investment

Where AI fell short

What needed humans

  • Thin grasp of user intent and nuance
  • Domain workflows & edge cases need validation
  • Generic personas, oversimplified flows
  • Quality swings with prompt and input quality
  • Misses business priorities and strategy
  • Struggles with complex workflows
  • Usability, accessibility, quality need oversight

What I'd do differently.

01

Write a tighter, feature-level brief upfront — most rework came from gaps AI filled for us.

02

Build the design system first, then generate screens against something stable.

03

Plan human-correction time as a phase, not a surprise.

04

Checkpoint for fast-moving tools — a major one landed at the buzzer.
