The problem

Finding the right people is harder than building the product

Building a startup is hard. Finding the right investors, mentors, incubators, and early teammates is often harder, and today it happens through scattered LinkedIn DMs, cold emails, and word-of-mouth, with no single place built for the startup ecosystem.

The brief: design a mobile community platform where every member of the ecosystem, founders, VCs, incubators, advisors, coworking spaces, media, can discover each other and form meaningful connections efficiently.

Why mobile?

Founders and investors live on their phones between meetings, events, and pitches. Networking is opportunistic, it happens in stolen moments: waiting for a call, commuting, at a demo day. A desktop-first product would miss exactly the moments where this behaviour happens. That single insight shaped almost every design decision that follows.

Research

Talking to the people who would actually use it

What I wanted to learn

  1. How does early-stage fundraising and sourcing actually happen today?
  2. Which platforms do people currently use to interact, and where do those fall short?
  3. What makes tracking a startup's status difficult for investors and incubators?
  4. How hard is it for founders to validate a concept and reach investors, VCs, and incubators quickly?

What the research surfaced

Discovery is the bottleneck, not interest

Investors want deal flow and founders want capital, they simply can't find each other efficiently.

Identity is fragmented

A founder might represent themselves and several startups at once. Existing platforms force a single identity.

Trust requires context

People won't connect over a name alone. They need a credible, complete profile before reaching out.

Time is the scarcest resource

Any flow longer than a couple of minutes gets abandoned, especially on a phone.

Challenges & opportunities

Four product opportunities, framed from research

Structure

Many-to-many association

One person can belong to multiple organisations; one organisation can hold multiple members. The data model mirrors how the ecosystem really works.

Community

A home for the whole ecosystem

A social space where founders connect with investors, incubators, advisors, and media, not just other founders.

Discovery

Structured, fast discovery

VCs and incubators find startups through filters and categories instead of cold search and referrals.

Effort

Effortless profiles

Creation and management of profiles in one place, because on mobile, every extra form field costs you users.

Process

From sticky notes to screens

The team ran card-sorting sessions to group features and define the information architecture. Card sorting mattered more than usual here: a phone screen can only surface four or five top-level destinations, so we had to be ruthless about what earned a place in the bottom navigation and what lived one level deeper.

I then sketched low-fidelity flows on paper before touching Figma, testing flow logic cheaply (taps to complete a task, what's visible without scrolling) before investing anything in visual design.

Solutions

What I designed, and why it works on a phone

Every screen decision is paired with the mobile principle behind it, annotated the way I'd redline a spec. The real screens for every solution are in the final designs gallery below.

01

Onboarding: one question per screen

Progressive disclosureTap over typeThumb zone

Sign-up is broken into small, focused steps. The key screen asks "How would you like to interact with the ecosystem?" with a simple checklist, startup team, investment, incubators, advisory, events, coworking, media. You can try this exact flow in the prototype at the top of the page.

Why it works on mobile
  • Progressive disclosure. One question per screen keeps cognitive load low and completion high, mobile users abandon long forms.
  • Tap, don't type. Large tappable rows replace free-text input. Selection is faster than typing and yields structured data to personalise the feed from day one.
  • Full-width primary action. The Continue button anchors near the bottom, inside the natural thumb zone, so progressing never requires a stretch.
02

A single, unified profile

One identityScroll over tabs

One profile represents a person across every organisation they belong to, founder of one startup, advisor to another, member of an incubator.

Why it works on mobile
  • One login, zero switching. Managing multiple accounts on a small screen means constant context-switching and repeated logins. One identity with attached organisations removes that entirely.
  • Scroll over tabs. The profile is a single scrollable page, scrolling is the cheapest gesture on mobile, far cheaper than tab-hopping for content this long.
03

A community feed with familiar patterns

Familiar patternsCard layoutThumb zone

A scrollable feed where members post updates, ask questions, and engage, with like, comment, and share on every card.

Why it works on mobile
  • Borrowed behaviour. The feed deliberately mirrors patterns people already know from LinkedIn and Twitter. On mobile, familiarity is usability, no one should need a tutorial.
  • Card-based layout. Content is chunked into units that fit the viewport, each with clear, separated touch targets.
  • Bottom tab bar. Home, Discover, Connections, and Profile sit at the bottom, the most-used destinations stay within one-handed thumb reach, following both iOS and Android conventions.
04

Startup creation: a long form, broken into chapters

ChunkingOne decision per screenTap over type

Creating an organisation is a multi-step flow, basic information → category → profile setup → funding details, each step on its own screen with a persistent progress indicator.

Why it works on mobile
  • Chunking. A startup profile needs a lot of data, but one long form on mobile is a guaranteed drop-off. Themed steps make each screen feel achievable.
  • One decision per screen. Every step ends with a single full-width Continue, never two competing actions.
  • Input matched to the medium. Dropdowns and pickers wherever options are finite; free text only where genuinely necessary, because the keyboard is mobile's biggest friction.
05

Connections & discovery built for speed

Glanceable cardsTouch targets

A Discover page where investors filter startups by category and stage, plus a Connections area for managing requests and messages.

Why it works on mobile
  • Glanceable cards. Name, category, stage, one-line pitch, just enough to make an accept-or-explore decision in seconds, matching the "stolen moments" usage pattern from research.
  • Fat-finger-proof actions. Accept and decline are large, separated buttons, so consequential taps can't misfire.
  • Integrated messaging. Follow-ups never leave the app, every external jump on mobile is a chance to lose the user.

Mobile principles used

Five ideas this design leans on

Tap each principle to expand it, the same tags appear on every solution above.

People hold phones one-handed, so the bottom third of the screen is easiest to reach. Primary actions and the tab bar live there, which is why every Continue button in this app is full-width and anchored low.

Show one question or task at a time instead of a long form. Onboarding and startup creation are built entirely on this, each screen feels achievable, so completion stays high.

Anything tappable should be at least ~44pt so fingers don't miss, hence full-width buttons, large checklist rows, and clearly separated accept/decline actions.

The keyboard is mobile's biggest friction. Wherever options are finite, selection inputs, checkboxes, chips, pickers, replace text fields, and the product gets structured data as a bonus.

The feed borrows learned behaviour from established social apps. On mobile, convention beats clever almost every time, no one should need a tutorial to use a feed.

Outcomes

Validation & what comes next

$500K raised by founders onboarding through the MVP, the business outcome the product was built to enable.
Chunked onboarding that people finished One question per screen replaced a wall-of-fields form, and usability testing confirmed users could complete sign-up and first profile unaided.
One identity, many ventures A unified profile resolved the “one person, many organisations” pain that surfaced in every interview, the structural bet the product runs on.

The $500K is the business outcome; the two cards beside it are the design decisions that made the product usable enough to get there.

Usability testing of the prototype confirmed that users could complete sign-up and create a startup profile quickly, validating the chunked-flow approach. The unified-profile model resolved the multi-identity pain point that surfaced repeatedly in interviews, and structured onboarding data enables a personalised feed from the very first session.

What I learned

  • Mobile design is subtraction. The hardest work wasn't adding features, it was deciding what didn't belong on each screen.
  • Research pays for itself. The "one person, many organisations" insight came directly from interviews and became the product's structural backbone. I would never have guessed it from intuition.
  • Conventions are a feature. I wanted novel interactions early on; testing taught me that on mobile, familiar patterns beat clever ones almost every time.

Moving forward

Next steps focus on smoothing registration for all ecosystem contributors, events, coworking spaces, media, deepening discovery filters, and validating the messaging experience with a larger beta group.
