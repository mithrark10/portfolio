What's at stake

In collections, these aren't cosmetic frictions. A wrong account pulled from weak search is a misapplied payment; a status no one can find is a compliance exposure; a slow agency round-trip is money sitting uncollected. The cost here is measured in dollars and regulatory risk, not clicks — which is exactly why the bar for clarity has to be this high.

Critique / Roads not taken

If I started over, here’s what I’d change

Strong work comes with a clear-eyed view of its own edges. With more runway, and fewer constraints from the system already in place, these are the directions I’d push, and the candid reason I held back on each. Tap to open one.

01Open on what needs attention, not a search box+
Why it could be stronger

Search assumes Robert already knows who he’s looking for. His deeper need is the opposite, “show me what’s gone wrong.” A role-based home that surfaces returned accounts, failed transactions and ageing batches would do his triage for him and cut the hunting entirely.

The trade-off I weighed

It only earns its place once there’s real data and real volume behind it. Launching that view empty, before we understood true usage, risked a hollow dashboard, so a search-first landing was the more honest first move.

02Treat status as a living pipeline, not a row of tabs+
Why it could be stronger

Tabs tell you where an account sits, but hide how work is flowing. A pipeline view, accounts visibly moving Submitted → In-Review → Accepted, would expose bottlenecks and let a banker feel throughput, which is closer to how they judge a good day.

The trade-off I weighed

Pipelines get unwieldy fast at high record counts, and the existing design system leaned on tables. Tabs shipped a clearer, more scalable v1, the pipeline is a strong, deliberate v2.

03Design bulk-first, because volume is the real job+
Why it could be stronger

Robert talks in plurals, “a few accounts and their transactions.” Yet the flows default to one record at a time, with bulk bolted on. I’d invert that: make multi-account import and creation the primary path, single-record the exception.

The trade-off I weighed

Bulk turns validation and error-handling into the hardest part of the UX, partial failures, row-level fixes, re-submission. Getting the single-record interactions right first let me earn the right to take that on.

04Collapse the client and agency views into one shared timeline+
Why it could be stronger

Today they’re two separate surfaces. The deeper fix for “where is this?” is a single source of truth both sides read, a shared account timeline of who did what, and when. Answer the question once, for everyone.

The trade-off I weighed

That opens real permissions and data-contract complexity across two different org types. Keeping the views distinct respected existing boundaries and shipped sooner, but a shared timeline is where I believe this product ultimately goes.

05Pressure-test the three pains with real bankers, not just stakeholders+
Why it could be stronger

My research leaned on three internal experts. They’re close to the problem, but they aren’t the user. With more time I’d run contextual interviews with real client reps, to confirm these are the right three pains, and to catch the ones no one on the inside can see.

The trade-off I weighed

Internal desk research was what the timeline allowed, and it was enough to move with conviction. But I’d treat every decision here as a hypothesis until a real Robert has had the chance to push back on it.

Getting it built

A surface for a user the product had never spoken to

For years Velosidy served agencies, not bankers. Proposing a brand-new client-facing surface meant making the case for a user who wasn't in the roadmap, and every decision below had a reason someone could say no to.

Pushback · "Why a new surface?"

The existing agency flow already worked. Why build bankers their own portal instead of extending what's there?

How I made the case

I anchored it in the three pains from stakeholder research and the cost of the agency round-trip, the wait, the re-keying, the blind spots, and made the case that the banker was a first-class user, not a guest in the agency's workflow.

Pushback · Extend the menus

The instinct was to bolt banker views onto the existing navigation rather than rethink it.

How I made the case

I pushed search-first, using the PM's own words back to the room, "people get lost in the menus." Finding an account had to take one query, not a drill-down.

Pushback · Scope

Bulk creation and import were wanted on day one, expanding the build before the core loop was proven.

How I made the case

I sequenced single-record first to validate the core loop, with bulk creation and import explicitly parked for the next horizon.
